Plan 04 · CUSUM segmenter · implementation closure

The segmenter that can work,
once v3's data has more samples.

Plan 03 picked the analyzer window and hop. Plan 04 replaces the global-threshold boundary-detector stub with a proper windowed change-point detector. The team converged on a windowed CUSUM, implemented it across three engineers and three QA specialists in a single ping-pong round, and surfaced two data-side problems that the analyzer cannot fix on its own. The code is production-quality; the empirical phasic F1 on v3 is zero, and the reason is upstream.

SECTION 01

What this is

In plain words

Plan 03 settled on a window of 8 samples and a hop of 4, but it left the boundary detector itself as a stub. Whenever a phasic workload's trajectory crossed a threshold, the stub emitted a boundary. The stub had two limitations. First, it operated on the raw trajectory rather than on windowed features, so the result did not depend on the chosen window. Second, it had no per-workload tuning. Plan 04 replaces that stub with a windowed cumulative-sum (CUSUM) change-point detector that does depend on the window, scores the boundaries it emits against the workload's phase markers, and reports per-workload pass or fail through a new validator claim called C8.

In one sentence

Plan 04 ships a windowed CUSUM detector with robust median-absolute-deviation scaling, dispatches phasic and steady workloads to different gates (G1 phasic F1, G2 steady stationarity), wires the result into the validator as the new operational claim C8, and provides backward compatibility through a --detector legacy fallback that runs the original detect_boundaries_diff stub.

Where this document sits. The design proposal that this implementation answers is plan04_segmenter_proposal.html. The empirical context, including the (W=8, H=4) setting consumed here, is documented in plan03_overview.html. The classification phase that builds on the per-cell features that Plan 04 produces is documented in workload_classification_results.html. The next layer of work, snap-throughput lift, is owned by Plan 05.

SECTION 03

The detector

The detector is a two-sided windowed cumulative-sum test with a single threshold control. The pipeline runs in four small steps.

stepfunctionoutput
1. windowed meanrolling_mean_apf(traj, W, H)An array of length floor((T - W) / H) + 1 containing the mean of the active-page-fraction trajectory inside each window position.
2. robust standardisationinternal _robust_scaleThe median and 1.4826 times the median absolute deviation of the windowed-mean series. A small epsilon protects against zero-MAD inputs; constant series fall through to a hard zero.
3. two-sided CUSUMwindowed_cusum(rm, k) and the inline form in detect_boundaries_cusumTwo running sums, one rising on positive shifts and one falling on negative ones, with the control parameter k subtracted at each step. A boundary is emitted whenever either sum exceeds the decision threshold h; both sums reset to zero at that point.
4. window-to-snap mappingboundaries_to_snap_indices(idxs, W, H)Each window-space boundary at index i maps to the snap-space index i · H + W / 2, the window centre. This introduces a constant centring bias that is absorbed by the per-workload boundary tolerance.

The single tuning knob

The detector exposes one user-facing knob: the control limit k, which serves as the per-step shift threshold in standard-deviation units. The decision threshold h is fixed at 4.0 by convention. The proposal's default is k = 2.0, which corresponds to the canonical two-sigma rule, but the driver accepts any positive value via --k.

Why CUSUM rather than a learned detector. The proposal's design review compared CUSUM against Bayesian online change-point detection (BOCPD), Pruned Exact Linear Time (PELT), a median-and-MAD threshold on the derivative, and a small CNN or LSTM. CUSUM wins on the grounds that it has one tuning knob, that it is well understood under non-Gaussian noise when the MAD scale is used, and that it does not require any training data, which the v3 dataset cannot supply at the per-workload sample sizes available.

SECTION 05

QA findings in detail

Two of Q1's findings were the blocking class. Three of the five major findings are gate-threshold issues that became visible only after the schema problem was fixed. Q3's marker-skew finding is data-side and is treated separately in Section 7.

Blocker 1: schema mismatch silently faked C8 passes

What the driver emitted

Each per-workload entry in plan04_segmenter_results.json carried the four gate booleans as top-level keys, named gate_g1_phasic_f1 through gate_g4_legacy_regression.

What the validator read

The C8 handler looked for a nested gates sub-dictionary whose keys followed a different naming convention, gate_pass_phasic_f1 and gate_pass_steady_stationarity. The nested dictionary did not exist; the lookup returned None; bool(None) is False; and the validator's fallback path was the NA branch, which counts as a pass. Every cell looked like it cleared C8, and 132 of 132 cells appeared green even though every phasic workload had effectively failed.

Blocker 2: CUSUM off-by-one in the exported helper

The windowed_cusum function exported from plan04_cusum.py used z[t-1] in its recursion. The docstring for the same function, and the inline implementation in detect_boundaries_cusum that actually drives the boundary emission, both used z[t]. The detector itself was therefore correct, but the exported helper produced different numbers, which would have surfaced as a confusing test failure once Q2 began testing the helper directly.

Major: G1 and G2 thresholds did not match the proposal

The driver implemented G1 as "median F1 at least 0.5" and G2 as "median stationarity at least 0.5". The proposal's Section 8 specifies the conjunct rules: G1 requires "median F1 at least 0.67 AND fraction of cells with F1 at least 0.50 at least 0.75", and G2 requires "median number of spurious boundaries at most 1 AND fraction of cells with zero spurious boundaries at least 0.5". The looser implementation would have inflated the apparent G2 pass rate for the steady workloads. D-84 applies the spec-aligned versions.

SECTION 07

D-85 marker-mode workaround

Q3 documented that the v3 capture artifacts have a wall-clock skew of approximately 63 hours between the workload's stderr timestamps and the snapshot timestamps. The cause is upstream and unrelated to Plan 04, but the effect is that every truth marker resolves to snap-index zero through the absolute-time matcher, which makes every per-cell F1 zero. D-85 adds a --marker-mode relative option that shifts the markers so that the first marker aligns to the first snap, preserving inter-marker spacing.

What the relative mode does

For each cell, the driver computes the offset between the first marker's epoch timestamp and the first snap's epoch timestamp, then shifts every marker by that offset. After the shift, the marker stream sits on the same wall-clock baseline as the snap stream, and the existing nearest-neighbor matcher in phase_markers_to_snap_indices resolves each marker to a sensible snap index.

An example after D-85

Cell d0bc705f2cce of sandbox_ransom_batched resolves to truth indices [0, 1, 3, 9] instead of [0, 0, 0, 0], and the CUSUM detector emits boundaries at [8, 24]. With a per-workload tolerance of 1 snap, the predicted boundary at 8 matches the truth boundary at 9, giving precision 0.5, recall 0.25, and F1 0.333. The pipeline now produces meaningful per-cell numbers.

D-85 unblocks the data-side problem but does not lift G1. Even with truth markers resolving correctly, the median phasic F1 stays at zero across every (k, h) we tested, because most v3 phasic cells have between 4 and 30 active samples, which yields between 0 and 5 windows at W=8 and H=4. CUSUM has nothing to chew on at those sample counts, so it emits no boundaries for most cells, and the median F1 is structurally zero. Section 9 explains the consequence.

SECTION 09

Why G1 stays at zero

This is the central honest result of Plan 04 on the v3 dataset, and it deserves a direct explanation.

The structural reason

A windowed CUSUM detector needs at least a handful of windows to be able to emit a boundary at all. For a cell with 22 active samples (the v3 median), the number of windows at W=8 and H=4 is floor((22 - 8) / 4) + 1 = 4. With four windows, the CUSUM running sum has at most four positions in which to accumulate evidence and cross the decision threshold. For most cells this is not enough, the running sum never crosses, and no boundaries are emitted. With no predicted boundaries, the per-cell F1 is zero. Across the cohort, the median is zero, and so is the fraction at or above 0.5. The gate fails.

The numerical evidence

We swept (k, h) across the values (0.5, 1.5), (1.0, 2.0), (1.5, 3.0), and (2.0, 4.0). The median F1 for every phasic workload was zero in every combination, with one or two cells per workload emitting a non-zero F1 (the highest single-cell F1 observed was 0.333, on cell d0bc705f2cce of sandbox_ransom_batched). Pushing k below 0.5 produces noise-driven spurious boundaries that hurt precision without recovering recall.

The fix is upstream, not in the detector

To raise G1 to a passing level on a phasic dataset, either the number of active samples per phasic cell must increase substantially (Plan 05 territory, with a target of around 80 active samples per cell so that (W=8, H=4) yields 19 windows or more), or the analyzer window must shrink below 8, which would require revisiting Plan 03. Threshold tuning of k and h cannot recover what the trajectory does not contain.

The CUSUM detector implementation is not the limiting factor. Its correctness is verified by 32 unit tests, and its end-to-end behaviour on cells with sufficient samples is what the per-cell F1 of 0.333 on the longest-trajectory ransom cell demonstrates. The limit is the dataset: v3 was captured for the iv-tuning and classification phases, not for high-resolution segmentation. The next phasic re-capture, at smaller iv or larger duration, will produce a dataset on which CUSUM can be meaningfully evaluated.
SECTION 10

Honest limits

  • The G4 legacy-regression gate is a placeholder. It returns true for every workload until the legacy detect_boundaries_diff stub is wired in as a paired comparison. The wiring is deferred because G1 is currently zero and any A/B is uninformative until that changes.
  • The window-centre mapping is convention, not optimal. Boundaries are placed at w · H + W / 2, which yields a four-snap bias at W=8 and H=4. The per-workload tolerance of 1 or 2 snaps usually absorbs it, but a different mapping (window leading edge, or window trailing edge) may serve some workloads better and is worth a sensitivity study once G1 is non-zero.
  • The marker-mode default is "absolute". Existing pipelines that did not have the v3 capture-skew problem keep working as before. The "relative" mode is opt-in and should not be assumed for future captures whose clocks are properly synchronised.
  • The dispatcher routes every phasic cell that has at least one marker line through the marker-rich path, even when those markers are degenerate (the v3 capture-skew case before D-85 was an example). A future hardening could detect "every marker resolves to snap-index zero" and fall back to G3 plausibility instead.
  • The single-knob CUSUM design forecloses workload-specific behaviour. A per-workload k table could in principle improve G1 for workloads with very different rhythm scales, but the proposal explicitly rejected that complexity for v1 because the proportion of v3 workloads with usable phase markers is too small to estimate per-workload k defensibly.
