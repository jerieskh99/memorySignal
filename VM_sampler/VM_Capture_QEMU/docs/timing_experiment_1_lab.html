In one breathThe pause-the-video analogy

Imagine watching a film while someone keeps hitting pause every few seconds to photograph the screen. The film's running time is unchanged — characters age at the same rate, scenes last the same number of minutes. But your evening gets very long because every pause adds real seconds to the wall clock without advancing the story.

The guest VM is the film. The host orchestrator is the photographer. pmemsave is the photograph. Every snapshot pauses the film for ~1–2 host-seconds, takes the photo, then unpauses. The film's timeline (Axis A) is short and contiguous from the guest's perspective. The photographer's evening (Axis B) is long and full of waiting.

Every later confusion about timing reduces to "which clock are we reading right now". The analyzer must read the film's clock (Axis A) when assigning periods to spectral peaks; the operator reads the wall clock (Axis B) when estimating how long an experiment will take.

Why it matters · technicalWhich axis controls which knob

simple terms

Axis A is what your analysis measures. Axis B is what your patience measures. Get them mixed up and a 0.1 Hz workload rhythm looks like a 0.007 Hz rhythm (or vice versa).

spectral

For a cepstral peak at frame index k, the period in physical time is k × Δt. The correct Δt is Axis A (= intervalMsec + ~25 ms). Using Axis B (the per-snapshot wall-clock cost) inflates every period by ~15× on this host.

Worked example5 minutes of guest activity

guest duration = 5 min · intervalMsec = 100 ms

Question: how many snapshots, and how long on the host?

snapshots = guest_duration / guest_dt = 300 s / 0.125 s = 2 400 snapshots host wall-clock = snapshots × host_dt = 2 400 × 1.50 s = 3 600 s = 60 min host multiplier = host / guest = 60 / 5 = 12×

So a 5-minute guest workload costs 1 hour of operator patience on this host. Choose your intervalMsec and your guest duration with the multiplier in mind.

Switch intervalMsec to 1 s, same guest duration:

Same guest content, but coarser temporal resolution (rhythms faster than ~2 s become invisible) and 9× less wall-clock. This is the operator's lever; whether 1 s resolution is enough depends on the workload's rhythm of interest.

Decoupling Axis A and Axis B = freedom to trade resolution against throughput.

The ruler above is a map of rhythms, drawn in guest time. Left edge is fast (a few milliseconds per cycle); right edge is slow (minutes per cycle). Why bars flipThe two levers that control every bar

Each bar is recomputed live from the sliders above. The math behind one bar is:

Per-workload classification at the current configuration
workload rhythm period T (guest seconds) ← FIXED per workload current Δt_frame = intervalMsec + 25 ms ← LEVER #1 (left-side sliders) current window (W) = window_size × Δt_frame ← LEVER #2 (analyzer config) if T < 2·Δt_frame: bar = aliased (Nyquist violation) elif T ≤ W/4: bar = clean (≥ 4 cycles in window) elif T ≤ W: bar = marginal (1–4 cycles) else: bar = invisible (rhythm > window)

Only two things you can change move bars: (1) intervalMsec, which shifts Δt_frame (and therefore the entire window length in guest time), and (2) window_size, which scales the window directly. Try it: at default intervalMsec=100, window=128, sandbox_ransom_batched shows invisible. Push intervalMsec to 1000 — the window becomes 128 s of guest time, comfortably wider than batched's 60 s rhythm, and the bar flips to clean. This is the per-family intervalMsec recommendation in visible form.

Geometry · caseWhy the fill width is "cycles per window"

The fill width on each bar is literally the ratio W / T capped at some upper bound for display. A bar at "full width" means the window contains the workload's rhythm many times over — easy to detect, easy to characterise. A bar at "tiny sliver" means the workload's rhythm needs many windows just to fit one period — impossible to detect inside a single window.

That makes the bars an honest discriminability proxy. Two workloads whose bars are both full-width but at different green shades still separate; two workloads with both bars at slivers are doomed regardless of model choice.

To map a specific scenario: at intervalMsec=100 the window is 16 s. io_seq_fsync's rhythm is 1 s → bar shows W/T = 16 cycles → full green. sandbox_ransom_batched's rhythm is 60 s → bar shows W/T ≈ 0.27 cycles → tiny red sliver. Even though the workloads are structurally very different, their bars converge to the same visual signal at the broken configuration: "incomplete information, classifier can't tell".

Build the intuitionWhat "discriminability" actually means

Before we can say the four mechanisms multiply, we need a clear picture of what they multiply against. The thing being eroded is called discriminability: how much numerical distance separates the feature vectors of two distinct workloads.

Imagine each workload produces a point cloud in feature space — many windows per recording, each contributing one point. Two well-separated workloads produce two cleanly-distinct clouds with a wide gap between them. The classifier draws a boundary in that gap. Two confused workloads produce overlapping clouds; no boundary can cleanly separate them.

Discriminability geometry
CLEAN CASE DEGRADED CASE CONFUSED CASE ████████ ········ ·········· ███A███ ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ░░·A·░░░ ▓░░░░░▓ ·A·▓·B····· ████████ ▓▓B▓▓▓▓ ··░░░░· ▓▓▓B░▓░ ··░░░·░░░░░ ▓▓▓▓▓▓ ░░░·░ ·░░░▓▓▓ classifier guesses clear gap margin narrows 50 % of the time accuracy ~100 % accuracy ~80 % accuracy ~50 %

Each mechanism we're about to describe takes a knife to the gap. Mechanism (i) collapses both clouds toward the middle. (ii) shifts both clouds in the same wrong direction. (iii) blurs each cloud outward. (iv) inflates the noise floor under both. None of these alone wipe out separation entirely. Together they squeeze the gap until the classifier can't find a boundary.

The point of saying "multiply" is that we cannot fix three of them and expect 75 % recovery. Each surviving mechanism keeps a multiplicative chunk of the damage. The chain has to be unbroken end to end.

Plain languageThe four mechanisms don't add — they multiply

With the gap-geometry picture in mind: imagine each mechanism survives some fraction f of the gap that existed before it acted. A perfect pipeline preserves f = 1.0. A mechanism that halves the discriminability has f = 0.5. If you stack four mechanisms with survival fractions fi, fii, fiii, fiv, the gap that reaches the classifier is the product fi · fii · fiii · fiv.

Numerical example: a workload pair that loses 60 % of its margin to mechanism i (so fi = 0.4), another 40 % of what's left to ii (fii = 0.6), and another 30 % to iii (fiii = 0.7) keeps 0.4 × 0.6 × 0.7 = 0.168 ≈ 17 % of its original discriminability. By the time the classifier sees the data, two genuinely distinct workloads look ~83 % identical.

That is the difference between a classifier you can interpret and a confusion matrix you can't trust. Each individual mechanism looks survivable on its own — fix the worst one and you'd expect a clean dataset. The product math says otherwise: even one surviving mechanism with f = 0.3 caps your recoverable signal at 30 %. Fixing only some of them leaves a low ceiling.

With A's rhythm at 10 s, we need a window of at least 4 × 10 = 40 s in guest time to land cleanly. Working backwards:

required window in guest seconds W_need = 4 × T_A = 4 × 10 s = 40 s window size in frames W = 128 (Phase 1 canonical) required Δt_frame Δt = W_need / W = 40 / 128 ≈ 0.31 s required intervalMsec iv = Δt − 25 ms overhead ≈ 285 ms nearest "nice" round number 250 ms

The cost: each window now covers more guest seconds, so we need a longer guest run to collect the same number of windows, plus each second of guest time costs less host wall-clock (good — see §I subsection 4).

Why not intervalMsec = 500 ms or 1 s instead? Those would push deeper into the clean band (5.3 and 10.6 periods/window respectively) but at a cost: a small workload-internal sub-rhythm at, say, 1 s would now be near the Nyquist edge at intervalMsec = 500 ms (2·Δt = 1.05 s = T) and outright aliased at 1 s. 250 ms is the smallest intervalMsec that pushes A's main rhythm clear of the marginal band while still keeping ~0.5 s sub-rhythms safely above Nyquist. Goldilocks choice for this pair.

Orientation · read this firstWhat the four moving parts actually are

This investigation has four kinds of moving parts. They look similar from outside and do completely different things. Conflate them and the timeline stops making sense. A test is not a plan; a plan is not a round; a bug is not any of those. Each has one job in the loop.

1 · Tests are measurements

Tests observe the pipeline. They run the producer, collect timestamps, and write a JSON. They do not change the pipeline. Their job is to make defects visible — not to fix them. There are four of them in this investigation.

  • sanity — one instrumented producer run. "Does it tick at intervalMsec?"
  • 2a — A/B with consumer on vs off. "How much variance is the consumer's fault?"
  • 2b — sweep intervalMsec across 4 cells. "What's the pause-fraction at each interval?"
  • 2c — A/B with flush on vs off. "Is the 0.5 s flush necessary?"
technical

Each test is one Python orchestrator that drives the producer (and optionally a consumer) with controlled parameters and parses the JSONL stream into a summary JSON. The orchestrator does not modify the producer; it only sets env-vars and reads the resulting timestamps.

The JSON is the artifact of record. Per the audit chain, "no JSON, no claim". Each round's analysis cites JSON field names, not console output.

2 · Bugs and mechanisms are what tests find

Two letter classes, kept separate on purpose:

  • Mechanisms i–vii (lowercase Roman) — theoretical hypotheses for why the classifier confused workload pairs it should have separated. Each is "something the analysis chain could be doing wrong."
  • Bugs D–H (uppercase Latin) — concrete engineering defects in the orchestration code. Not hypotheses; observed failures.

Tests do not fix bugs. They make bugs measurable. Once measurable, a bug gets a plan.

technical

Mechanism IDs are independent of plan IDs by design. Two reasons: (a) one plan may close several mechanisms; one mechanism may need several plans. (b) Mechanisms map to hypotheses in the original confusion-matrix analysis (pre-investigation), so they outlive any particular fix.

Bugs D–H were not in the original hypothesis space. They were discovered while fixing mechanisms. They got their own letter class so a reader of an old report knows D is not in the original 7.

3 · Plans are code patches

4 · Rounds are verification cycles

A round is a full re-run of the relevant tests after one or more plans land. Round N tests the pipeline as it stands after plans up through round N have committed.

Five rounds (R1 → R5) plus two pre-investigation single-test runs (Run 1, Run 2). Each round produced one JSON per test it ran. Each round either confirmed plans worked OR surfaced a new bug that needed its own plan.

A round is not a new investigation. It is a re-measurement under known conditions. The pipeline is the black box; the JSON is the evidence.

technical

Round granularity: re-run only the tests touched by the plans that landed. R4 only ran sanity + 2c because Plans 1c·5 only touched those. R5 only ran 2c because Plan 6 was 1 LOC in 2c. This keeps verification cheap (~1–10 min per round vs ~30 min for a full sweep).

Round status is appended to the existing JSON archive; old rounds are not overwritten. sanity_v3.json + sanity_v4.json both exist; the analysis cites the most recent.

Zoom-in · what was tunedWhat "n" is, and what Plan 6 actually changed

Plan 6 is the smallest plan in the entire investigation — one line of code — and its parameter (--stable-n) is the most subtle to explain in plain language. Worth unpacking on its own because it answers the question "what got tuned around Round 5?"

What n is

In the 2c test, each of the two passes (flush-on, flush-off) produces 21 snapshots. Each snapshot has a measured host wall-clock cycle time. To decide whether the 0.5 s flush sleep matters, the orchestrator averages these times for each pass and reports the delta.

n is the number of snapshots, counted from the start of each pass, that get included in that average.

In the JSON, it shows up as comparison.mean_host_snapshot_cycle_sec_first_n.n_used. CLI flag: --stable-n.

technical

n is the truncation length on the per-pass records list before the mean is computed. The 2c orchestrator computes both an all-snap mean and a first-n mean and writes both to the JSON. The verdict logic uses the first-n value; the all-snap mean is for audit only.

Implementation: stable_mean(records, key, n) in run_exp2c_flush_sensitivity.py. Plan 3 introduced the function; Plan 6 changed its default n from 10 to 5.

The runner analogy

Imagine timing a 21-lap runner who is fast at the start and tires later:

  • Laps 0–4: clean form, ~10 s each
  • Lap 5: form starts to slip
  • Laps 6–9: cramping, ~30 s each
  • Laps 10–20: walking, erratic

If the question is "how fast can the runner go when conditions are right?", you average the first 5 laps, not all 21. All-21 measures fatigue, not capability.

For 2c, "fatigue" is dirty-page pressure (next box). n=5 averages the clean window before the pressure distorts the measurement.

technical

This is a classic stationarity argument. The full 21-snap distribution is mixed-regime: a clean steady-state regime (snaps 0–4) followed by an I/O-pressure transient (snap 5+). The first-moment estimator (mean) is biased when applied to a mixed regime; a truncated estimator on a stationary sub-interval is unbiased.

n is effectively a regime-truncation hyperparameter. Choosing it correctly is choosing the right window of stationarity.

Why the late snaps drift

Plan 5 turned the producer's TIMING_SELF_CLEAN OFF for 2c, so that the integrity probe can find dumps to inspect. The side effect: each pmemsave leaves a 1 GiB file on disk. The Linux kernel queues these for asynchronous page-cache writeback.

By snap 6, the kernel has 5+ GiB of dirty pages waiting to flush. Every new pmemsave blocks on disk writeback, jumping from ~0.8 s to ~7–8 s. The 0.5 s sleep in flush-on mode gives the kernel time to flush between snaps, so flush-on never accumulates the pressure.

So host_snapshot_cycle_sec on snaps 6+ is not measuring "flush cost". It is measuring "dirty-page saturation cost". Averaging over those snaps contaminates the answer.

technical

Dirty-page writeback is governed by vm.dirty_ratio / vm.dirty_background_ratio. On the capture host the defaults (20 % / 10 % of RAM) start synchronous writeback once dirty bytes exceed the ratio.

With 1 GiB per pmemsave and roughly 1 s between snaps in flush-off, the dirty-page count grows by ~1 GiB/s. On a host with 16 GiB RAM, the 10 % background-writeback threshold is hit by snap 1–2, and the 20 % synchronous threshold by snap 3–4. Snap 6+ stalls in balance_dirty_pages() until the writeback catches up.

Plan 5's accidental side-effect re-introduced mechanism vi in only the flush-off pass — the same drift the producer self-clean was designed to prevent.

Bug G · the wrinkleWhy 2c's verdict flipped sign

Round 4 measured the opposite of Round 3 for the same flush comparison. Both readings are correct — they measured different disk-pressure regimes. The "real" answer was inside the same JSON the whole time.

In plain English

Plan 5 turned off the producer's auto-delete so the integrity probe could find some dumps to look at. That worked — probe is now happy. But: with auto-delete off, dumps pile up on disk during the pass. The 0.5 s flush sleep, which Round 3 measured as pure overhead, turns out to quietly buy the kernel time to write dirty pages to disk. Take it away and the second pass falls into a tar pit: snap 0–4 still take 0.78 s, then by snap 6 each pmemsave is taking 7–8 s because it's queued behind page-cache writebacks from previous snaps.

So the flush is doing two things at once — it's overhead when dumps disappear quickly (production / Round 3), and it's a disk-pressure shock-absorber when dumps stick around (Round 4 with Plan 5). Round 4 measured the second case by accident.

technical

The two passes were measured under different dump-survival models. Pass A (flush_on): sleep 0.5 after each pmemsave lets the kernel asynchronously flush dirty pages → pmemsave stays at ~0.84 s for all 21 snaps. Pass B (flush_off): no flush → dirty-page count grows monotonically → pmemsave_sec goes 0.78, 0.78, 0.81, 0.79, 0.78, 1.95, 7.25, 8.27, 7.91, 8.08, 6.00, 3.04, ... → I/O-blocked on writeback. With Plan 5's TIMING_SELF_CLEAN="", mechanism vi re-emerged in flush_off only. The flush masks mechanism vi as a side effect.

In production the consumer process drains dumps as fast as the producer writes them, so the model is structurally equivalent to Round 3's self-clean ON. The production-case verdict therefore remains REMOVE the flush — Round 3's measurement was the load-bearing one.

Plain languageWhat each test actually does

The chain has three small tests (2a / 2b / 2c). Each isolates one specific question. None of them touches data, and none of them touches the existing capture pipeline when it is not running. They are diagnostic probes, not part of the capture chain.

Test 2a · the "kill the noisy roommate" test

Runs the producer twice for 60 s each. First with whatever the host state happens to be ("consumer on"). Second after pkill'ing the consumer ("consumer off"). Compares per-snapshot timing between the two passes. The question it answers: how much of the host-side variability is the consumer's fault?

technical

A/B comparison of host snapshot cycle distribution under two states: (1) consumer subprocess running and consuming queue, (2) consumer killed + queue drained. Producer-only timing in both. Reports paired means / medians / std / pause-fraction. Mechanism iii diagnostic.

Test 2b · the "how slow can I sample" test

Runs the producer four times in a row, once for each of intervalMsec ∈ {100, 250, 500, 1000} ms. Each cell is 60 s of host wall-clock. The question it answers: how much does the VM spend paused as we slow down sampling — and where's the sweet spot?

technical

One-factor parametric sweep over intervalMsec, RAM held constant. Each cell measures the pause fraction (Σ paused / Σ wall-clock). Output is a monotonic curve; the operator picks an intervalMsec per workload family using this curve plus that family's rhythm-of-interest. Mechanism iv diagnostic.

Test 2c · the "do we still need this brake?" test

The producer has a hard-coded sleep 0.5 after every pmemsave — a safety brake from early development. 2c runs the producer twice (with the brake on, with the brake off) and checks if the dumps are still readable in both. The question it answers: is the 0.5 s safety brake actually doing anything, or is it just slowing us down?

technical

Toggles TIMING_NO_FLUSH env var that gates the post-pmemsave sleep 0.5 in the producer. Compares host_dt distribution and dump integrity (file size + 3-point readability probe) between flush_on and flush_off. Reports a KEEP / REMOVE / NEUTRAL verdict. Cheap throughput win or "keep it for safety" decision.

Plain languageWhat each plan actually changes in the pipeline

Four small code changes. None of them adds a feature; all of them close a previously-identified gap. None touches the analyzer; all four are in the capture / orchestrator side.

Plan 1 · "let the producer take out its own trash"

When the consumer is killed for timing isolation, no one is left to delete old dump files. Plan 1 adds an opt-in toggle: when TIMING_SELF_CLEAN=1 is set, the producer deletes the previous dump as soon as it has written the next one. Like the consumer's rolling-delete, but cooperative.

technical

Patch to capture_producer_qemu_pmemsave.sh: when env var TIMING_SELF_CLEAN is set, replace the queue-enqueue branch with sudo rm -f "$prevImage". Steady-state on-disk dump count stays at 2 (prev + curr). Eliminates mechanism vi (in-pass pmemsave drift). ~6 lines bash.

Plan 1b · "tell the orchestrators to use it"

The producer change in Plan 1 is opt-in. Plan 1b makes 2a / 2b / 2c each set the env var when they launch the producer, so the new behaviour is automatic during timing experiments without affecting normal captures.

technical

env["TIMING_SELF_CLEAN"] = "1" in each orchestrator's producer-launch subprocess.Popen environment. Three files × ~3 lines.

Plan 3 · "compare the clean part, not the drifty part"

2c's verdict was wrong because it averaged across 21 snapshots — including the last 10 which had drifted. Plan 3 makes the verdict use only the first 10 snapshots, where pmemsave is stable. Keeps both numbers in the JSON for transparency.

technical

Add stable_mean(records, key, n=10) helper to run_exp2c_flush_sensitivity.py. Recommendation logic uses mean_first_10 instead of mean_all. Both values reported in JSON. ~20 lines Python. Fixes mechanism vii.

Plan 4 · "stop crying wolf on disk space"

The disk-free pre-check assumes every dump stays on disk for the whole run. After Plan 1 the producer cleans as it goes, so peak disk use is two dumps — tiny. Plan 4 makes the warning aware of that.

technical

Add peak_concurrent_dumps parameter to disk_free_check. Orchestrators pass 2 when self-clean is on, the snapshot count when it's off. Stops a false-positive WARNING that was misleading and made operators question valid runs. ~10 lines Python.

With everything stable, the 2c verdict logic finally has clean data to read — fix it fourth. Reverse the order and each step is debugging on a foundation that's still moving.

After Round 5 landsWhat unblocks

Mechanisms i–vii + D + E + F + G are then closed end-to-end. The capture pipeline produces measurable, calibrated data with a clean audit trail (7 plans landed across 4 commits). Three things become possible:

  • Plan 02 · per-family intervalMsec profile. Use the pause-fraction sweep (R2: 92/85/76/59 % at iv=100/250/500/1000) plus each workload's defining rhythm to pick the right Δt per Phase-2 family. Mechanism iv becomes a knob, not a bug. This is the actual thesis result.
  • Plan 03 · window / hop tuning. With Δt now correct, the canonical (128, 64) can finally be validated against actual workload rhythms instead of against an artefact of sleep 0.
  • Plan 04 · k-segmentation sweep. The final tunable in the chain. Defer until classifier behaviour on re-collected data is in.
  • Re-collection of confused workload pairs. The dataset gets the right calibration; the classifier sees properly-shaped features for the first time. Drop the 0.5 s flush in production for ~10 % wall-clock saving on every Phase-2 capture.

If after all that the classifier still confuses pairs that should separate, the next suspect is no longer the capture pipeline — it's feature definition, normalization, or the classifier itself. That is the clean diagnostic outcome this whole experiment chain was designed to produce.
